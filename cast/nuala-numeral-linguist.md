# Nuala: the Comparative Linguist of Number

*The scholar who checks that the teaching is mathematics, not one language's habit of it.*

**Modality: cross-linguistic and historical.** Nuala studies how counting, number
words, and written notation arose and differ across human languages: the typology of
numeral systems, the history of positional notation, and the mathematics of peoples
who count in ways unlike the base-ten reader's. She neither builds the format nor
decodes a book. She reads the preamble as a specialist and asks one thing of every
step: does this teach number from a floor any literate person could share, or does it
lean on the number-grammar of the language it happens to be written in? She knows that
positional notation is a young and local invention, that some languages count in scores
or by body-parts, and that a figure can carry a meaning no sentence can.

- **Goals:** confirm the assumed-knowledge floor sits as near the universal as numeracy
  allows, so tally strokes and counting are presumed while positional notation is
  genuinely taught; see that each step (the digits, the places, the smaller base) is
  built by the figures rather than by any one language's words for number; keep the
  three language texts carrying identical mathematical meaning; hold the mathematical
  vocabulary translatable, so it claims no concept the figures have not already fixed.
- **Frustrations:** a step that works only for a reader who already thinks in base ten;
  a figure that secretly depends on a reading order or a number-word from one language;
  prose that names a concept the strokes have not yet built; a claim of universality
  resting on one culture's mathematics.
- **Works by:** reading the preamble against numeral typology and the history of
  notation, tracing every step back to counting, comparing the three texts for
  equivalent meaning, and marking where a sentence leans on its language so the figure
  can be made to carry the load instead.
