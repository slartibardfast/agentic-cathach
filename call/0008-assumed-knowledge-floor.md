# The assumed-knowledge floor: tally strokes

- Status: accepted
- Amended: call/0013 records that the budget's arithmetic (addition and multiplication) is taught from this floor, never assumed.
- Scope: cathach
- Date: 2026-07-04

## Context and Problem Statement

The preamble teaches a reader to decode the book, and how much the reader is presumed to
know sets where the teaching begins. This is the deepest question of the format. The choice
is between presuming positional numerals, writing for a reader who already reads numbers,
and teaching counting up from the beginning.

## Decision

The floor is tally strokes. A reader is presumed only to see that marks carry meaning and to
count marks one by one. Everything above that is taught, from strokes up: first the digits,
by pairing each with a row of strokes, then the places, by grouping strokes into tens, then
the arithmetic of the checks and the repair.

The reasons:

- A durable artifact assumes the least, because the reader is unknown and may be far in the
  future. Counting is the most universal numeracy. Positional notation is a later invention
  a reader may not share, so it is taught, never presumed.
- The cost is a page or two of careful teaching, paid once in each language, carried mostly
  by language-independent figures.

## Consequences

- Sections 3 and 4 of the preamble build from strokes. Nothing about numbers is assumed.
- The figures carry the teaching, so the floor holds across the three languages.
- The floor is not lowered below counting. A reader who cannot count marks is outside the
  format's reach, and that limit is accepted.
